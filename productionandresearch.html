Machine Learning in Production

Updated 19 October 20214 min read comments

ML in production is quite different from research. Objectives in research are very different from production. As VentureBeat reports, around 90 percent of machine learning models never make it into production. In other words, only one in ten of a data scientist's workdays actually end up producing something useful for the company. Since ML usage in production is very new , so if you are going to deploy your ML first time, beware you might have a culture shock. We are going to compare the difference between ML in research and production on the basis of their objective, Computational priority, Data, Fairness, Interpretability.

Objective

Research and production projects have very different objectives. The most common objective in research is to obtain the highest model performance- model achieving a state of the art results on benchmark datasets. Even improving a small change in performance can make a model in research very complex. Those complex models suffer from several problems like overfitting, low interpretability, etc.

There can be many stakeholders involved in putting the ML model into production which each having its own unique objectives. For example - consider a company making object recognition software can have the following stakeholders:

  1. Computer Vision Engineer who designs complex CV-based models for object recognition comparable or higher than SOTA models.
  2. Product Manager wants to maximize the profit
  3. Cloud Engineer who handles the cloud infrastructure hosting models in production
  4. Product team who are concerned that models should infer results in real-time and have lower latency and faster inference.
  5. Lastly, the sales team to make more revenue through the service.

It’s very hard to arrive at a model which satisfies all the stakeholders and yet all will have to collaborate to arrive at a model to fulfill every stakeholder’s objective.

Computational Priority

While most of the ML engineers in the research focus entirely on developing models, training ML models is a computationally expensive task. After training, the model can be used for inference on the test set to obtain results.

In research, training is the bottleneck since obtaining SOTA performance is the key objective and thus researchers aim for faster training. While in production, testing is the bottleneck thus faster inference is prioritized.

Latency and Throughput

Latency indicates how long it takes for packets to reach their destination. Throughput is the term given to the number of packets that are processed within a specific period of time. Throughput and latency have a direct relationship in the way they work within a network. Latency is measured in units of time - hours, minutes, seconds, nanoseconds, or clock periods. Throughput is the number of such actions executed or results produced per unit of time. Research prioritizes high throughput whereas production prioritizes low latency.

In terms of Image Classification:

Throughput is the number of images processed in one second for batch size > 1.

Batch size is the number of images processed at a time together.

Example of Throughput: GoogleNet for Image Classification classifies 297 images in one second on Intel CascadeLake. This improves to 904 images in one second if we use INT8 version of GoogleNet on the same system for the same application. Hence, the Throughput is 297 images/ second or 904 images/ second.

Latency is the time taken to process one image for batch size 1.

Batch size is the number of images processed at a time together.

Example of Latency: GoogleNet for Image Classification takes 0.057 seconds to classify one image on Intel CascadeLake. This improves to 0.009 seconds if we use INT8 version of GoogleNet on the same system for the same application. This means the user has to wait for 0.009 seconds to get the result for 1 image.

Data

Data Available in research is often preprocessed, clean, and well-formatted free from any types of noise. In fact, there are lots of benchmark open-sourced datasets that researchers use to train different models. In production, the story is quite different right data is difficult to find. Data if available is either messy, unstructured, or dynamically shifting. If annotated labels are available, are sparse, imbalanced, outdated, or incorrect and business requirements might require adding another label class or merging two existing label classes.

Thus, to sum up, data in research is constant, cleaned, noise-free while in production one can expect to work with data that is being constantly generated by users, systems, and third-party data.

Fairness

During the research, researchers don’t worry much about fairness.Data so used in training can have some biases thus leading to biased results during inference. For example, Your loan application might be rejected because the ML algorithm picks on your zip code, which embodies biases about one’s socio-economic background. When ML systems are productionized at a larger scale, it can induce biases and can hurt the sentiments of people.

Interpretability

Deep learning is thought to be a black box that just takes input and churns output. Interpretability is very important for ML models for users, both business leaders, and end-users, to understand why a decision is made so that they can trust a model and detect potential biases mentioned above. Second, it’s important for developers to debug and improve a model.

In conclusion, production and research are two sides of ML and there are many problems that a ML engineer will have to think through before putting ML model into production.
